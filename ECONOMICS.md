# ECONOMICS

The key question is whether a free audit tool can generate qualified pipeline cheaply enough to justify operating it as an ongoing product. Because I do not have Credex’s exact gross margin numbers, I am using rough but defensible assumptions. Suppose a converted customer buys discounted AI credits or related tooling support worth roughly $8,000 annually in gross revenue. If Credex captures a 15 percent effective margin on that relationship, the gross contribution is around $1,200 per converted customer per year. If repeat expansion or multi-tool purchasing happens, that number could be meaningfully higher, but I would rather underwrite the model on a conservative assumption.

From the channels in the GTM plan, CAC should vary a lot. Organic posts on X, Indie Hackers, and Hacker News can be treated as near-zero direct CAC but high time cost. Warm manual outreach has higher labor cost but better targeting. If I price founder or operator outreach time at roughly $40 per hour and assume one hour produces 8 meaningful messages with one audit completion, then manual outreach CAC might be around $40 per completed audit from that channel. Community posts and launch content might produce audit completions at under $10 each on a blended basis if the content lands. The important point is that this tool should not require paid acquisition early; if it does, the economics get much harder quickly.

The core funnel math starts at audit completion. Assume 100 completed audits produce 25 saved reports. Of those 25, maybe 8 show more than $500 in monthly savings. If 30 percent of those high-savings users book a Credex consultation, that gives about 2 to 3 consultations per 100 completed audits. If 25 to 35 percent of consultations convert into a credit purchase, then 100 completed audits produce roughly 0.6 to 1 customers. At $1,200 gross contribution per customer, that means each audit completion is worth perhaps $7 to $12 in expected gross contribution under conservative assumptions. That is enough to justify a low-cost organic distribution engine, but not enough to justify sloppy paid acquisition.

To make the tool meaningfully profitable, the big lever is not traffic; it is the share of audits that surface real overspend and the conversion rate on those high-savings cases. If the tool becomes accurate enough that high-savings users trust it, then “audit completed to consultation booked” matters more than generic top-of-funnel volume. A useful benchmark would be: 100 completed audits, at least 10 with more than $500 monthly savings, 4 booked consultations, and 1.2 customers closed. At that level, the product is not just generating leads; it is generating leads with strong buying intent.

For this tool to contribute to $1M ARR in 18 months, the math has to be explicit. If one converted customer is worth $8,000 ARR, then Credex needs roughly 125 net new customers attributable to this motion. Spread across 18 months, that is about 7 customers per month. If the conversion rate from completed audit to customer is 1 percent, the tool needs roughly 700 completed audits per month. If the conversion rate improves to 2 percent on a better-qualified traffic mix, the requirement drops to about 350 completed audits per month. So the real challenge is not “can this page get traffic?” It is “can the audit be good enough, and the audience targeted enough, that a completed audit becomes a high-quality buying signal?”
